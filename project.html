The project

A pilot for collecting and linking Greek and Arabic medical access forms.

Goal

Ancient Medical KnowBridge sets out to build a navigable interface that records, side by side, the techniques premodern scholars used to organise and retrieve medical knowledge. Long before search engines, the writers of Greco-Roman Egypt had their own ways of segmenting, labelling, classifying and querying what they knew — lists, definitions, classifications, tables, recipes, branching diagrams, and above all the question-and-answer catechism (κατήχησις, later ἐρωταπόκρισις). This site treats those forms as a small, structured collection rather than as scattered curiosities.

The starting material is a thesis chapter on catechistic medical papyri — brief teaching manuals copied out in second- to fourth-century Egypt, from Faiyum to Oxyrhynchus, on subjects from the pterygium of the eye to hydrocephalus, apoplexy and sciatica. Each papyrus is read for the access forms it embodies, and each form is then bridged to its counterpart in the later Arabic medical tradition. The aim is not a new edition of the texts but a clear, inviting way to see how a manuscript page can become a research interface — and to make dense classical scholarship feel approachable to a curious non-specialist.

Principles

Four commitments shape every page of the prototype.

Evidence-based

Every claim, date and quotation is anchored to a named witness — a specific papyrus, edition or author. Where the papyri are damaged or a reading is uncertain, the gaps are kept visible rather than smoothed over, just as the chapter keeps its lacunae and conjectures in plain sight.

Source-grounded

The Greek shown on the site is drawn only from the papyri and the authors quoted alongside them — Oribasius, Pseudo-Galen, Paul of Aegina, Aëtius of Amida — each paired with a translation. Arabic material appears strictly as an illustration of the parallel tradition, never as a quotation invented for a particular manuscript.

Interoperable

Forms, papyri and sources are modelled as linkable items so they can point outward — to standard sigla and MP³ numbers, to digital editions, and eventually to manuscript images. The intent is that each entry can resolve to a stable record elsewhere rather than living only inside this site.

Open & collaborative

This is a pilot meant to grow. It is built to be read, corrected and extended — by papyrologists, historians of medicine, Arabists and digital humanists alike — so that more forms, more witnesses and more traditions can be folded in over time.

Roadmap & next steps

The prototype is deliberately small. These are the concrete, honest next steps that would turn it into a genuinely useful scholarly resource.

StepWhat it involves
1 Link each papyrus to its edition Connect every catechistic papyrus to its entry on papyri.info (and its MP³ number), so that P. Aberd. 11, P. Ross.Georg. I 20, P. Ct Y-BR, P. Genav. inv. 111 and P. Mil.Vogl. I 15 each resolve to a citable digital record.
2 Add IIIF manuscript images Where digitised plates exist, link out to IIIF manuscript images so a reader can compare the printed text against the surviving fragment, rather than relying on transcription alone.
3 Add real Arabic witnesses The Arabic examples on the site are currently illustrative of the parallel tradition. The next step is to cite real witnesses by name — works such as Ibn al-Bayṭār's Kitāb al-Jāmiʿ li-mufradāt al-adwiya, Ḥunayn ibn Isḥāq's al-Masāʾil fī al-ṭibb, and the aqrābādhīn (compound-drug) genre — with proper editions in place of the placeholders.
4 Extend beyond ophthalmology Reach past the five catechistic papyri and their cluster of eye, head and chronic-disease topics, drawing in further didactic and surgical papyri so the collection reflects the broader spread of medical schools across Greco-Roman Egypt.
5 Add more examples of the seven forms Populate each of the seven access forms — lists, definitions, classifications, tables, recipes, Q–A and diagrams — with additional worked examples, so each form is shown across several texts rather than a single specimen.
6 Build search & filter Add a way to search and filter across the forms — by topic, by papyrus, by tradition or by date — so a reader can ask, for instance, "where does the surgical needle βελόνη appear?" and follow the answer across the collection.

Status & credits

Pilot prototype Digital humanities Research use only Work in progress

Ancient Medical KnowBridge is a pilot digital-humanities prototype, made for research and study use only. Its content is built from a thesis chapter on the catechistic medical papyri of Greco-Roman Egypt; the Greek quotations, translations, dates and sources on the site are drawn from that chapter and its cited editions.

It is a work in progress. Several details are still being refined — Arabic witnesses remain illustrative for now, outbound links to editions and images are still being added, and the collection covers only a first handful of texts. Corrections and contributions are warmly welcomed; this is meant to be a bridge that others can help build out.
