Sources & further reading

Everything on this site is built from a single thesis chapter on the catechistic medical papyri of Greco-Roman Egypt. Here are the primary sources behind it, the ancient and medieval authors it draws on, a short Greek glossary, and the public databases where you can begin to trace the manuscripts yourself.

Primary sources — the papyri

Five catechistic medical papyri — short question-and-answer teaching manuals — anchor the whole study. Each is a fragment of a sub-medical school's classroom material, scattered across the towns of Greco-Roman Egypt. The list below follows Table 5 of the chapter; the MP³ numbers are the standard catalogue references for literary papyri.

PapyrusProvenanceDateTopic
P. Aberd. 11 (= MP³ 2342) Faiyum 2nd century Pterygium and its surgical treatment
P. Ross.Georg. I 20 (= MP³ 2343) Unknown (likely Faiyum) 2nd century Glaucoma, staphyloma and pterygium
P. Ct Y-BR (Beinecke) Arsinoïte nome Late 2nd–early 3rd c. CE Hydrocephalus
P. Genav. inv. 111 (= MP³ 2373) Unknown 2nd–3rd c. CE Surgical instruments (treated in the chapter of surgery)
P. Mil.Vogl. I 15 (= MP³ 2340) Oxyrhynchus Mid 3rd–4th century Elephantiasis, apoplexy and sciatica

A sixth papyrus, P. Giss. Univ. IV, is cited in the chapter only in passing — as a surgical witness to the needle (βελόνη) in the treatment of coloboma — and so is not counted among the five catechisms above.

Ancient & medieval authors cited

The catechisms are brief, but their answers quote — sometimes verbatim — the great medical authorities. That is part of their importance: even a small provincial school in the Faiyum was teaching from the canon. These are the authors named in the chapter, with a one-line gloss on the role each plays in it.

AuthorRole in the chapter
Hippocrates (5th c. BCE)Earliest to describe hydrocephalus as excess water in the head, and first to treat it by ventricular puncture; his De morbis II describes apoplexy as a sudden stroke.
Asclepiades of BithyniaFounder of the corpuscular theory (ἄναρμοι ὄγκοι); P. Mil.Vogl. I 15 quotes his account of apoplexy and sciatica as obstruction of corpuscles in the pores of the nerves.
Celsus, De MedicinaRecords some forty kinds of sharp hook used for pterygium; recommends poultices and trephination for hydrocephalus, and treats apoplexy and paralysis as one weakening of the nerves.
Pliny the ElderReports that elephantiasis was unknown at Rome until Pompey's day, then came from Egypt — black spots on the face spreading to the bones.
AretaeusRecommends the purgative hiera picra (aloes and canella bark) and radish to procure vomiting in elephantiasis.
GalenHis pharmacology and his theory of the cerebrospinal fluid as spiritus animalis stand behind the papyri; the four-fold sorting of hydrocephalus follows the Galenic tradition.
Pseudo-Galen, Introductio sive medicusSection 19 classifies hydrocephalus into four types by where the fluid gathers — exactly as P. Ct Y-BR does.
Caelius AurelianusHis Latin On Acute and Chronic Diseases (Celeres passiones) preserves the fullest version of Asclepiades' corpuscular theory, closely paralleling the apoplexy answer in P. Mil.Vogl. I 15.
Oribasius, Collectiones medicaeNames κατήχησις as a teaching technique of memorising; attributes hydrocephalus in infants to the head being squeezed by midwives at birth.
Aëtius of AmidaHis sixteen-book medical encyclopedia (book VII, ophthalmology) describes removing the pterygium with hook, needle, linen and horsehair; he rejected thread-ligation of staphyloma in favour of the scalpel.
LeonidasWith Paul and Aëtius, describes draining hydrocephalic fluid by suture, but only when the water has collected outside the bone.
Paul of AeginaGives the detailed account of staphyloma ligation (ἀπόσφιγξις) and of the pterygotome; with Galen's school, recommends the thread method this papyrus uses.

The Arabic bridge

The chapter itself is purely Greek papyrology — it contains no Arabic text. But the project frames these Greek access-forms beside the later Arabic medical tradition that inherited and transmitted them. The authors below are the bridge, named here without quoting any specific manuscript.

Author / workWhy it matters here
Dioscorides, De materia medicaThe foundational Greek pharmacopoeia of simple drugs — the model for the long ingredient lists carried forward into the Arabic tradition.
Ḥunayn ibn Isḥāq, al-Masāʾil fī al-ṭibbThe "Questions on Medicine" — a catechistic teaching text in the very question-and-answer form (masāʾil) that mirrors the Greek κατήχησις, illustrative of the parallel Arabic tradition.
Ibn al-Bayṭār, Kitāb al-Jāmiʿ li-mufradāt al-adwiyaThe great 13th-century compendium of simple drugs (Leiden Or. 167); the Arabic anchor for the site's example lists and pharmacological tables.

The small Arabic examples shown elsewhere on this site are illustrative of the parallel Arabic tradition — they are not transcriptions of any one named manuscript. The aqrābādhīn (compound-drug) genre is named only as a genre, not quoted as a source.

A small Greek glossary

A handful of Greek terms recur across the catechisms — some name the teaching method itself, others the eye diseases and instruments the papyri describe. Glosses are drawn directly from the chapter.

TermMeaning
κατήχησις (katḗchēsis)Catechism — teaching by word of mouth; from the verb κατηχέω, "to teach orally." Named by Oribasius as a technique of memorising medical facts.
ἐρωταπόκρισις (erōtapókrisis)The middle-Byzantine name for the same question-and-answer format, from ἐρώτησις (questioning) + ἀπόκρισις (answer).
πτερύγιον (pterýgion)Pterygium — a membranous outgrowth from the corner (canthus) of the eye spreading over the cornea; the diminutive of πτέρυξ, "wing," so a "little wing."
σταφύλωμα (staphýlōma)Staphyloma — a growth near the pupil "like a grape," from σταφύλη, "bunch of grapes."
ὑδροκέφαλον (hydroképhalon)Hydrocephalus — moisture collected in the head; from ὕδωρ, "water," and κεφαλή, "head."
ἄγκιστρον (ánkistron)The surgical sharp hook used to isolate the pterygium from the eye (Latin hamulus acutus).
βελόνη (belónē)The surgical needle — attested across four papyri, the key witness for instrument vocabulary; threaded with linen and horsehair in the operations.
ἀπόσφιγξις (apósphinxis)Ligation — tying off the base of a staphyloma with thread, described in detail by Paul of Aegina.
ὑποσπαθισμός (hypospathismós)Hypospathismos — a procedure making incisions through the skin of the forehead and passing a spatula beneath, used for chronic eye disease and chronic migraine.
ῥευματισμός (rheumatismós)Ophthalmic secretion or fluxion (also ῥεῦμα / ῥυάς) — one of the four humoral flows; given as a cause of both cataract and staphyloma.

External databases

These are open scholarly databases, maintained outside this project, where you can begin to locate the actual manuscripts, their editions and their digital images. They are starting points for tracing the papyri listed above — each opens in a new tab.

papyri.info Trismegistos Leuven Database of Ancient Books (LDAB) Wikimedia Commons
DatabaseWhat it offers
papyri.info The central aggregator of papyrological editions and metadata — search by publication sigla (e.g. P. Aberd. 11) to reach texts, apparatus and links.
Trismegistos An interconnected registry of ancient texts; gives each papyrus a stable TM number that ties together its many sigla, provenance and dating.
Leuven Database of Ancient Books (LDAB) A catalogue of ancient literary manuscripts — useful for situating these medical teaching texts among the wider book culture of Greco-Roman Egypt.
Wikimedia Commons A repository of public-domain images, including manuscript and papyrus photographs that can be reused freely with attribution.

These are external resources maintained by others and are offered here only as starting points for locating the manuscripts; the editions and dates above follow the thesis chapter.
